This was not addressed to Scrooge, or to any one whom he could see, but it produced an immediate effect. For again Scrooge saw himself. He was older now; a man in the prime of life. His face had not the harsh and rigid lines of later years; but it had begun to wear the signs of care and avarice. There was an eager, greedy, restless motion in the eye, which showed the passion that had taken root, and where the shadow of the growing tree would fall.  He was not alone, but s at by the side of a fair young girl in a mourning - dress: in whose eyes there were tears, which sparkled in the light that shone out of the Ghost of Christmas Past.  “It matters little,” she said, softly. “To you, very little. Another idol has displaced me;  and if it can cheer and comfort you in time to come, as I would have tried to do, I have no just cause to grieve.”  “What Idol has displaced you?” he rejoined. “A golden one.”  “This is the even -handed dealing of the world!” he said. “There is nothing on wh ich it is so hard as poverty; and there is nothing it professes to condemn with such severity as the pursuit of wealth!”  “You fear the world too much,” she answered, gently. “All your other hopes have merged into the hope of being beyond the chance of its sordid reproach.
